# Preference Modeling vs. Binary Cross Entropy

Your intuition on Binary Cross-Entropy (BCE) is structurally correct:

$$
\mathcal{L}_{BCE} = -[ y \log(p) + (1-y) \log(1-p) ]
$$

Where $y$ is the ground-truth binary label (1 or 0), and $p$ is the model's predicted probability, typically generated by $p = \sigma(logit)$.

If we framed our Reward Model as predicting "probability that $y_w$ is better than $y_l$", we could map this to BCE where the ground truth label is always $y=1$ (since we construct the dataset such that the chosen response is always positive). The BCE loss would simplify to:

$$
\mathcal{L} = -\log(p)
$$

### The Core Question: Why $\sigma(r_w - r_l)$ and not $\sigma(r_w) - \sigma(r_l)$?

You asked why the paper uses:
$$
\mathcal{L}_{RM} = -\log(\sigma(r_w - r_l))
$$
instead of maximizing the gap between probabilities. 

This relies entirely on the **Bradley-Terry (BT) Model of pairwise comparisons**. The BT model posits that the probability that item $w$ beats item $l$ is determined by their intrinsic scalar strengths (their latent "rewards"):

$$
P(w \succ l) = \frac{e^{r_w}}{e^{r_w} + e^{r_l}}
$$

If we divide the numerator and denominator by $e^{r_w}$, this algebraically simplifies to the sigmoid of their difference:

$$
P(w \succ l) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-(r_w - r_l)}} = \sigma(r_w - r_l)
$$

Therefore, $\sigma(r_w - r_l)$ **is literally** the exact predicted probability $p$ that the human prefers $w$ over $l$ under the BT model framework.

When we plug that predicted probability $p$ into our simplified BCE objective ($-\log(p)$ where the true label $y=1$), we arrive perfectly at the InstructGPT formulation:

$$
\mathcal{L}_{RM} = -\log(\sigma(r_w - r_l))
$$

### Summary
We are **not** just maximizing a raw scalar gap. The sigmoid of the scalar difference *represents the formal probability* under the Bradley-Terry ranking model. 

* The larger the difference $r_w - r_l$, the closer the probability $\sigma(r_w - r_l)$ approaches 1.
* We take the negative log of that probability to compute the cross-entropy penalty, exactly as you would in standard classification.
